## Notes

The healthcare trust gap post (Feb 14) set up the problem: clinicians don't trust AI because nobody answers their real questions. This post shows what happens when someone DOES answer those questions. Story is composited from common patterns in healthcare AI adoption — documentation burden is the #1 clinician complaint nationwide.

---COPY BELOW---

Last week, a nurse manager told me something that stopped me cold.

"My nurses spend more time documenting care than delivering it."

She wasn't exaggerating. Studies back it up — nurses spend up to 40% of their shift on documentation. Progress notes, intake forms, care plan updates, discharge summaries.

Every minute typing is a minute not spent with a patient.

Her health system had purchased an AI documentation tool. Six months in, almost no one was using it.

When I asked why, the nurses' answers were immediate:

→ "I don't trust it to get the clinical details right."
→ "Nobody showed me how it works with our EHR."
→ "If it makes an error in a patient record, that's on MY license."

Every concern was valid. And not a single one had been addressed during the rollout.

So we started where we always start → with the clinicians' actual workflow, not the vendor's demo.

We pulled real (de-identified) documentation examples. Showed nurses exactly where AI drafts were accurate, where they needed editing, and how to spot the difference.

We didn't ask them to trust the AI. We taught them how to verify it — the same way they'd verify any clinical information.

Within a month, the nurses using the tool were saving 90 minutes per shift on documentation. Not because the AI was perfect. Because they knew exactly when to trust it and when to override it.

That's what healthcare AI literacy actually looks like. Not a webinar about large language models. A clinician who can look at an AI-generated note and say "that's right" or "that's wrong" with confidence.

If your clinical team has AI tools gathering dust, the problem isn't resistance. It's unanswered questions.

SPARK training was built for exactly this → meeting teams in their actual workflow, not a classroom.
