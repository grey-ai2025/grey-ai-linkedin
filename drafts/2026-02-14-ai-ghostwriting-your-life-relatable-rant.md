## Notes

Washington Post published a study (Feb 2) showing that using AI to write personal messages leaves people feeling guilty and fraudulent. Psychology research confirms: recipients react more negatively when they learn a message was AI-generated, especially when personal effort was expected. This is trending TODAY — Valentine's Day.

The workplace parallel is the entire point: when you let AI replace your thinking instead of support it, the output feels hollow — and eventually, people notice.

---COPY BELOW---

ChatGPT can write your Valentine's Day card.

It'll be eloquent. It'll rhyme if you ask. It'll hit all the right emotional beats.

And it'll feel like absolutely nothing.

A Washington Post study found that people who use AI to write personal messages end up feeling like frauds. Not because the words were bad — because the words weren't theirs.

Here's what nobody's connecting:

This is exactly what's happening at work too.

That AI-drafted email to your team? Your manager can tell it's not your voice.

That AI-generated strategy summary? Your VP knows it doesn't reflect your actual thinking.

That client proposal ChatGPT "wrote for you"? It reads like every other proposal ChatGPT wrote for everyone else.

AI is incredible at giving you a starting point.

It's terrible at replacing the part that makes your work yours.

The people who use AI well aren't outsourcing their thinking. They're using AI to handle the structure so they can focus on the substance.

→ Let AI draft the outline. You add the insight.
→ Let AI summarize the data. You make the judgment call.
→ Let AI format the email. You write the sentence that actually matters.

The difference between AI as a tool and AI as a ghostwriter is the difference between sounding competent and sounding like everyone else.

This Valentine's Day, write your own card.

And on Monday, bring your own thinking back to work.

What's something you stopped letting AI write for you?
